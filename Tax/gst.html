What is GST? (Goods and Services Tax)

The full form of GST is Goods and Services Tax. It was first introduced in the Budget Speech presented on 28th February, 2006. It had the foundation for a comprehensive reform of India's indirect tax system. It was implemented on 1st July, 2017, as the Goods and Services Tax Act. It replaced several taxes levied by the Central and State Governments, simplifying the taxation structure. This landmark reform aimed at creating a unified national market and boosting economic growth. GST is a destination-based tax levied at all stages from manufacture to consumption, with credit of taxes paid at previous stages available as set-off. This not only avoids the cascading effect of taxes but also ensures that the final consumer bears only the GST charged by the last supplier in the supply chain. The GST regime is managed by the Goods and Services Tax Council, which is the governing body responsible for making recommendations on all matters related to GST, including tax rates, rules, and regulations. The implementation of GST has streamlined the tax collection process, reduced compliance burden for businesses, and improved transparency in the taxation system. It has also led to increased tax buoyancy and formalization of the economy. Despite initial challenges, GST has emerged as a transformative tax reform that has positively impacted the Indian economy. It is still evolving with continuous changes and updates being made to the laws and procedures to further simplify and improve the system.

GST Meaning and Scope

Goods and Services Tax (GST) is a destination-based multi-stage, comprehensive tax levied at each stage of value addition. Having replaced multiple indirect taxes in the country, it has successfully helped the Indian economy by creating a unified national market.

GST is levied on goods and services sold within India's domestic boundary for consumption, implemented by a majority of nations worldwide with respective customizations. The tax has been successful in simplifying the indirect tax structure and increasing tax compliance. GST is a consumption-based tax, which means that the tax is levied at the point of consumption rather than at the point of production. This ensures that the tax is collected by the state where the goods or services are finally consumed, thus promoting fair distribution of tax revenue among states. The GST framework is designed to be transparent and efficient, with online portals and digital systems for registration, filing of returns, and payment of taxes. This has not only reduced the compliance burden for businesses but also improved the ease of doing business in India. The GST Council, which is the governing body for GST, regularly meets to discuss and resolve issues related to GST implementation, ensuring that the tax system is responsive to the needs of businesses and taxpayers. History of GST and GST Information

Back in 2000, the then Prime Minister of India introduced the concept of Goods and Services Tax, he also formed a committee to draft new indirect tax law.

Following is the history of GST implementation in India:

  • 2000: Committee set up by the PM for drafting Goods and Services Tax law for India.
  • 2004: A task force suggested a need to implement the law and improve the indirect tax system in India.
  • 2006: GST was first introduced in the Budget Speech on 28th February 2006 by the Finance Minister of India.
  • 2007: Decision to phase out Central Sales Tax (CST).
  • 2011: Constitution (115th Amendment) Bill introduced in Parliament.
  • 2012: Constitution Amendment Bill passed by Lok Sabha.
  • 2013: Constitution Amendment Bill referred to the Standing Committee.
  • 2014: Constitution (122nd Amendment) Bill introduced in Lok Sabha.
  • 2015: Constitution Amendment Bill passed by Lok Sabha.
  • 2016: Constitution Amendment Bill passed by Rajya Sabha and assented by President.
  • 2017: GST Council recommended GST rules and rates.
  • 1st July, 2017: GST implemented in India.

GST is applicable only if there is a supply of goods and services. Before a business can determine its GST liability, assessing the following table about the Components of GST is essential:

State Goods and Services Tax (SGST) SGST refers to the tax payable on the sale of services and products within a state. It replaces previous taxes, including Value Added Tax, Entry Tax, State Sales Tax, Entertainment Tax, Luxury Tax, and Lottery Tax.
Central Goods and Services Tax (CGST) The tax levied on the supply of intra-state products is CGST. The Central Government regulates this tax.
Union Territory Goods and Services Tax (UTGST) This applies to the sale of products and services in Union Territories, such as Andaman and Nicobar, Daman and Diu, Chandigarh, Dadra, etc.
Integrated Goods and Services Tax (IGST) The sale of inter-state products and services leads to IGST. This is IGST which is collected by the Central Government, and is then shared equally between the states.

Understanding the Dual Structure of Goods and Services Tax

Since Goods and Services Tax is a destination-based tax, it is levied by both the Central and State Governments. This dual structure allows both the Centre and the State to levy and collect taxes.

Goods and Services Tax in India carries this same dual structure, thus having two components, state as well as a central component. This means that every transaction under GST will involve both a central and a state component, except for inter-state transactions which will involve an integrated GST. The dual structure ensures that both the central and state governments have a share in the tax revenue, thus promoting fiscal federalism. Concepts of GST Levy - Multi-Stage, Destination-based and Value Added

This comprehensive nature of the Goods and Services Tax levy takes into account every stage of manufacture and distribution, right from the purchase of raw materials to the final sale to the consumer.

The various stages of GST application are discussed below:

Meaning of Multi-Stage Levy

From production/manufacture to consumption, tax is levied at each stage of the supply chain, from the purchase of raw materials to the final sale to the consumer.

The different stages of production of a finished good and its corresponding rate in the market comprise the following stages:

  • Raw material purchase
  • Manufacture/production of raw materials into finished goods
  • Storage in warehouse/godown
  • Sale of goods to the wholesaler
  • Sale of goods to the retailer
  • Final sale to the consumer

GST charged at each link of this chain makes it a multi-stage tax.

Meaning of Destination-based Levy

A destination-based levy means the tax is to be taxed at the place where it is consumed and not at its origin. This means that the location where an item is consumed will rightfully collect the tax.

In this context, it is essential to differentiate the concept of 'supply' from 'place of supply'. The decision regarding whether a sale is interstate or intrastate will be based accordingly.

Meaning of Taxation on Value Addition

This concept of GST on value addition implies every addition made to an item to make it saleable to the end user is taxable under this regime. Understand it with the help of an example:

The manufacturing unit of Britannia Industries Limited in Chennai, Tamil Nadu, manufactures a variety of biscuits. During the manufacturing process, it goes through the following stages, with value added at each stage:

  • Processing flour, sugar and other materials into a dough and baking into biscuits.
  • The biscuits are then sent to the warehousing agent for storage and further processing.
  • The warehousing agent then sells the biscuits to a wholesaler.
  • The wholesaler then sells the biscuits to a retailer.
  • The retailer then sells the biscuits to the final consumer.

For each monetary value added to these stages, making the product saleable, GST is levied, thus making it a tax on value addition.

Composition Scheme under GST

The composition scheme under GST eases the process of indirect tax payment for small taxpayers. As per the CBIC, companies with an annual turnover within Rs. 1 Crore can opt for the scheme to pay their taxes. For North Eastern states, the threshold limit is Rs. 75 Lakh. Businesses under this scheme benefit from simplified tax compliance and lower tax rates. However, they cannot claim input tax credit and are restricted from making inter-state sales. The composition scheme is an optional scheme for small taxpayers to reduce their compliance burden and simplify the taxation process. They are required to file simplified quarterly returns and pay taxes at a fixed percentage of their turnover. This scheme is available for manufacturers, traders, and restaurants with a certain annual turnover. The government has also introduced a higher threshold limit for certain states to promote ease of doing business. Overall, the composition scheme is a beneficial option for small taxpayers to reduce their tax burden and simplify their tax compliance under the GST regime.

The tax rate applicable under the composition scheme includes the following:

Business type Rate of CGST Rate of SGST Total GST Rate
Goods Traders and Manufacturers 0.5% 0.5% 1%
Restaurants that do not serve alcohol 2.5% 2.5% 5%

You must, however, also meet a set of conditions to avail of the scheme. A taxpayer needs to file GST CMP-02 when opting for the scheme. This is a mandatory step to inform the tax authorities about their decision to opt for the composition scheme. The taxpayer is also required to issue a bill of supply instead of a tax invoice and mention 'Composition Taxable Person, not eligible to collect tax on supplies' on the bill. They also need to file quarterly returns in Form GSTR-4 and an annual return in Form GSTR-9A. It is important for taxpayers to carefully consider the pros and cons of the composition scheme before opting for it, as it may not be suitable for all businesses. They should also ensure that they meet all the eligibility criteria and comply with the rules and regulations of the scheme to avoid any penalties or legal issues. The government has also introduced various initiatives to promote the composition scheme and encourage small taxpayers to opt for it, such as providing online tools and resources for easy compliance. Overall, the composition scheme is a beneficial option for small taxpayers to simplify their tax compliance and reduce their tax burden under the GST regime.

Concept of Input Tax Credit (ITC) under GST in India

An Input Tax Credit (ITC) means that when a business person or a trader is paying tax on output, he/she can reduce the tax already paid on input (purchases).

For example, in the case of a manufacturer selling the final product, the output tax stands at Rs. 450. However, he already paid Rs. 300 as input tax while purchasing the product. He can thus receive an ITC of Rs. 300 on the final product and needs to pay only the difference of Rs. 150, i.e., Rs. 450 - Rs. 300 to the government as Goods and Services Tax.

ITC can be claimed only by businesses registered under the Goods and Services Tax Act. Also, all registered suppliers must be registered under the act for you to be eligible to avail of ITC.

Hence, go for Goods & Service Tax registration if you are eligible and not yet registered. Also, file Goods and Services Tax returns and pay your taxes within the due date to avoid any inconvenience.
