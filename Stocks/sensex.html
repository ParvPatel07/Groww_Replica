What is SENSEX?

Sensex is a portmanteau of two terms - Sensitive and Index, and was coined by Deepak Mohoni, a stock market expert.

What is Sensex?

Sensex is primarily used to denote the most popular market index of 30 companies listed under the Bombay Stock Exchange.

This component companies listed in this index today are some of the biggest companies in the country with the most actively traded stocks.

Companies included under it are selected by S&P BSE Index Committee based on the following five criteria -

  1. Companies have to be listed under the Bombay Stock Exchange in India.
  2. It must consist of large or mega-cap stocks.
  3. It has to be relatively liquid.
  4. It must generate earnings from core activities.
  5. Companies must contribute to keep the sector balanced with the country's equity market.

Ever since opening up in the 1990s, it has witnessed rapid growth, especially post 2000. For instance, in 2002, information technology companies formed the index, and in 2003, it was for the fast-moving. The growth curve can be traced to rapid rise in Gross Domestic Product (GDP), along with the stock market.

Some of the companies under this index include Axis Bank, Asian Paints, Bajaj Finance, Bharti Airtel, HCL Technologies, Hindustan Unilever, ICICI Bank, IndusInd Bank, Tata Consultancy Services, Larsen & Toubro, etc.

How is Sensex Calculated?

BSE maintains Sensex share composition from time to time to ensure that it reflects the current conditions of the stock market. At first, the index was calculated based on a weighted method of market capitalization.

However, since 2003, this calculation method was reformed and now integrates a free-float capitalization method.

This free-float method is an alternative of market capitalization method, where instead of a company's outstanding shares, the number of shares available for sale on the market is used to calculate the index. This means that only the shares that are available for public trading are considered, and the shares held by promoters and insiders are excluded.

This factor is the ratio of floated shares to outstanding shares. According to this free-float capitalization method, the index level accurately demonstrates the free-float value of the 30 listed companies under Sensex, relative to a base period.

How to Invest in Sensex?

These are the major steps that must be followed before starting your investment in Sensex:

  • Opening a Demat account

It is important to have a Demat account that holds your shares in electronic form.

  • Opening a Trading Account

After opening a Demat account, you should register yourself for opening a trading account. As BSE does not allow direct purchase or sale of securities. With the help of a trading account, one can easily buy and sell securities online.

  • Having a Bank Account

Other than having a Demat and trading account, an investor must also have a bank account and PAN card to trade on Sensex.

Milestones of Sensex Index

The following table illustrates the gradual rise (and fall) of Sensex index through India's stock market history -

Timeline Events
Early 80's to the end of the 20th century Ever since its integration, Sensex touched 1000 on 25th July 1990 and closed at 1004.
1992 saw the introduction of various liberal policies which led to Sensex index crossing 3000 for the first time in 1992.
In 1992, Harshad Mehta scam led to unrestricted selling of Sensex shares.
In 1999, the index crossed the 5000 point mark for the first time, ushering in the new century.
Beginning of the 21st century to mid-2000s Onset of the 21st century brought a boom in the market thanks to IT, which pushed the index to 6000 points. This record was held until 6 years, till 2nd Jan 2006, when Sensex had 9394 points.
In 2000, Sensex crossed 7000 points for the first time, owing to settlement in the equity market, which led to uncomfortable gains for the Bombay group of companies.
Between June and December 2005, the index witnessed rapid growth and crossed 8000 points, owing to brisk purchasing from foreign institutional investors, as well as domestic mutual funds.
Mid 2000s to its end On 7th February 2006, the index, during mid-session touched a high of 10,000 points.
The period between 2006 and 2007 saw a leap in Sensex index growth due to aggressive purchases of funds. It jumped from 10,000 to 20,000 points in December 2007.
The period between 2008 and 2010 saw market fluctuations with stock market crash. It then started recovering, and on November 5th, 2010, it closed at 21,004.98 points, crossing 21,000 points.
2013-2015 In October 2013, Sensex index closed at 21,033.67 points, ushering in a new high for the index.
In 2014, its closing index was higher than the Hang Seng Index, leading it to become Asia's highest value stock market index. It also witnessed a rapid increase from 21,000 points to 28,000 in the same year, breaching the 28,000 point mark in 2014.
On the 23rd of January 2015, the index also crossed 29,182.95 points, posting it at a new high. Later, the index also crossed 30,000 points and 31,000 points for the first time.
2016-2017 In 2016, the index crossed 32,000 points for the first time, and in 2017, it crossed 33,000 points.
2018-2019 In 2018, the index crossed 34,000 points for the first time, and in 2019, it crossed 35,000 points.
2020-2021 In 2020, the index crossed 36,000 points for the first time, and in 2021, it crossed 37,000 points.

Therefore, in the past 2 decades, even through India's stock market has undergone bullish and bearish trends, the Sensex has overall been positive for investors with a steady growth of Sensex.

Major Plunges in Sensex Stocks

The stock market history holds a few crashes between 2008 and 2009, with a fall in the Dow Jones Industrial Average, as well as various trading activities of stock market crash.

This crash also affected India's stock market adversely and led to a loss of 1600 points on 21st January 2008, which was its highest since its inception. The next day, the index went into a downward spiral with trading suspended for an hour.

From January to November 2008, the index continued to drop constantly, throwing the entire market into uncertainty. In October 2008, the market closed at 8509.56 points, its lowest in the last 15 years.

Again, in 2015, the index dropped by almost 750 points due to Kalyan jewel, which threw the market into turmoil.
