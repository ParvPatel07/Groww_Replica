Capital Gain Index

To understand the capital gain index, first, it is important to understand what is a capital gain and the overall concept that runs behind such index.

Capital gains are associated with gains that have been generated through the sale of capital assets. Proceeds acquired through such transactions are often either through government-regulated means or commercial investments, such as stocks, mutual funds, or bonds.

Capital gain index is a tool that helps to track the process of acquiring/selling of these assets while also being helpful for other factors.

For individuals and investors like equities, preference shares, UTI, non-equities bonds and debentures, gold, and real estate, the capital gain index is an important calculation.

Besides that, the total price of assets should be more than 36 months (the asset(s) should be treated as a long-term asset).

Capital Gain Index Calculation

The gains earned on selling capital assets would be regarded as long-term capital gains if assets are held for more than 36 months and capital gain index is used.

When it comes to determining if such capital gains to be generated from a transfer, individuals need to adopt a capital gain index for calculation and reporting purposes.

What is Indexation?

Indexation is a process that enables individuals to protect their earnings against the erosion of value caused by inflation.

The process involves adjusting the purchase price of an asset for inflation with the help of a price index. This adjustment increases the purchase price of the asset, thereby reducing the capital gain and, consequently, the tax liability.

Indexation is particularly beneficial for long-term capital assets, as it helps in reducing the tax burden on gains arising from the sale of such assets. The government notifies the cost inflation index (CII) every year, which forms the basis for indexation.

When it comes to Mutual Funds, the Cost Inflation Index is used to adjust the purchase price of acquisition, thereby reducing the capital gains and, consequently, the tax liability.

Indexation and Debt Funds

Indexation is especially beneficial for debt funds, as it allows long-term capital gains to be taxable. Investors in debt funds can take advantage of indexation to reduce their tax liability, as capital gains generated through investment returns are taxed at a lower rate after indexation is applied.

Concept of Cost Inflation Index and Capital Gain

There are various factors in indexation and capital gain. Therefore, it is also advisable to take the same into account when calculating one's tax liability in India for long-term capital assets and capital gains.

Typically, the Cost Inflation Index (CII) is a parameter of inflation. Large amounts of inflation in the index calculation is done by taking base cost inflation index figures during the determination of index.

The index is to calculate the long-term gains and capital gains in other words.

The following is the stepwise approach to calculate capital gain index value for a sale of an asset in question.

An individual who wants to have a steady and cheap property cost of acquisition with the cost inflation index as for the financial year after the purchase of such property, the same is simple.

For example, if an individual has invested in a house that was bought in the year 2002-03 for Rs. 4,50,000, and the asset was sold in 2016-17 for Rs. 21,00,000, the indexed cost of acquisition would be calculated as follows:

Indexed cost of acquisition = (Cost of acquisition × CII of year of sale) ÷ CII of year of purchase
(Rs. 4,50,000 × 1125) ÷ 105 = Rs. 48,21,429

Benefits of Indexation in a Nutshell

The difference between the cost price of an investment and its current market value is the amount of capital gain an individual would acquire on the event of a sale.

Here are a few benefits of indexation of a capital gain that individuals can avail themselves:

  • With the help of indexation, individuals would be able to minimize the impact of inflation on their debt funds and would be adjusting it to current price accordingly.
  • Individuals would be able to reduce their share of long-term capital gains. Eventually, bringing down their taxable income.
  • For instance, select funds investors would be able to earn better returns as the CII (Cost Inflation Index) in their assets would turn tax-efficient. The Fixed Deposit holders who are paying higher amount of tax than their returns are taxed would be able to offset this as the long-term returns of an individual based on the higher indexation is reduced.

For example, individual A, invests in an FD at Rs. 15,000 at a rate of 7% p.a., by the end of 5 years –the maturity value would accrue at Rs. 21,000 and the income accrued would be counted as Rs. 7,000.

Now suppose, individual A, if his value after the index is to be marked to 50%, after actual tax amount of Rs. 3,500 would be paid if the price after exchange and sale.

Hence the overall tax paid is lower than what would be incurred on debt fund after indexation.

Make sure to use the cost inflation index to be able to make a more compact capital gain index calculation. Besides the accuracy, individuals are offered the opportunity to gain a reasonable return as indexation also enables and encourages the lower tax liability on the part of the exchange and sale.

However, to be able to make the most of the CII, individuals needs to be aware of the current index value for capital gains and refer to the latest Cost of Inflation Index.
