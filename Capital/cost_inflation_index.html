Cost Inflation Index

The world economy is dynamic and subject to constant change. This change is evident from a decrease in the purchasing power of money due to sustained rise in the prices of goods and services.

The phenomenon of reduction in the value of money, which leads to an increase in an individual's cost of living, is known as inflation.

Cost Inflation Index or CII is a tool used in the calculation of an estimated yearly increase in an asset's price as a result of inflation.

The Central Government fixes the index and publishes it in its official gazette for measuring inflation. This index, notified each year by the Government is notified under Section 48 of the Income Tax Act, 1961.

What is the Purpose of CII?

A cost inflation index is used to calculate the long-term capital gains from a transfer or sale of capital assets. Capital gains relate to the profit acquired from the sale/transfer of any capital assets, including land, property, stocks, shares, trademarks, patents, etc.

In accounting, usually, long-term capital assets are recorded at their cost price in books. Thus, despite the rising prices of assets, these capital assets cannot be revalued.

Thus, at the time of sale of these assets, the profit or gain acquired from them remains high as there is no gain in price compression (purchase value remains constant, but sale value increases). Hence, there may be a higher increase in the gain amount, which should ideally be curbed or reduced.

With the application of cost inflation index for capital gains in the long run, the purchase of assets is adjusted according to the inflation percentage. As a result, capital gains are lower, and the tax payable is also reduced.

The Central Board of Direct Taxes in February 2018 notified new Cost Inflation Index numbers applicable from 2017–18 onwards. It is relevant, in particular, for those who have acquired capital assets after 1st April 2001. The base year for the new CII is 2001, with 100 as the value for 2001–02. The index value for all the years after 2001–02 is also notified by the government.

What Does a Base Year in CII Mean?

The base year is the year for which the price in this index value is set at 100. The calculation of yearly inflation/gain is done in accordance with base years so that it shows the increase in the inflation percentage.

For capital assets purchased before the base year of CII, assessees can consider their purchase price as the higher value between the actual cost or the fair market value as of 1st April 2001. The base year is thus the benchmark to which capital assets purchased long ago are compared, so that their FMV is calculated based on the latest valuation report or prevailingly a registered valuer.

How is Indexation Applied for Long-Term Capital Assets?

Indexation is applied to the cost of asset acquisition to adjust the price of an asset in accordance with inflation.

Following is the formula to calculate the indexed cost of asset acquisition:

Indexed Cost of Asset Acquisition =
(Cost of asset at the time of asset acquisition) × (CII for the year the asset is being sold or year 2024-25, whichever comes earlier) ÷ (CII for the year of asset acquisition)

Following is the formula to calculate the indexed cost of asset improvement:

Indexed Cost of Asset Improvement =
(Cost of asset at the time of asset improvement) × (CII for year during which the asset improvement took place) ÷ (CII for the year of asset improvement)

Example of Application of Indexation for Long-Term Capital Assets

Suppose you purchased an asset, and the capital gain is measured at Rs. 2,00,000. The Fair Market Value of the capital asset as of 1st April 2001 was Rs. 2,20,000. If the asset is proposed to be sold, select the amount that is higher between the actual cost and FMV as of 1st April 2001.

Following is a calculation of the indexed cost of asset acquisition –

Indexed cost of acquisition = (Rs. 2,20,000 × 348) ÷ 100 = Rs. 7,65,600

Therefore, the cost of acquisition of this asset is Rs. 7,65,600.

Therefore, the cost of acquisition of this asset is Rs. 7,65,600.

Things to Note about Cost Inflation Index India

  • If an asset is received as an assessee's will, CII is considered for the year in which it is received. In this case, the asset's actual purchase year has to be ignored.
  • Any improvement cost incurred before April 1st 2001 is not liable for indexation.
  • Benefits of indexation are not applicable for debentures or bonds, apart from RBI issued sovereign gold bonds or capital indexation bonds.

How Can Indexation Reduce Tax Liabilities on LTCG for Assessees?

Assessees can bring down their quantum of tax applicable on long-term capital gains acquired from the transfer of assets like debt mutual funds, real estate, etc., by adjusting their total indexed amount in accordance with the CII for each year, and then deducting it from the sale amount.

Tax, accordingly, gets acquired by an assessee from the transfer or sale of a property will attract relevant capital gains taxes, whether long or short term. The holding period changes the rate of tax applicable. For long-term capital gains, a standard rate of 20% with CII application.

When CII is applied to your computation, the quantum of gain is substantially reduced. Thus, the amount on which indexation benefit is available is lower, and the tax amount is also lower.

This indexation and the liability of tax is the primary reason for assessees to choose the techniques and features of bond and fixed maturity plans (FMPs). The application of the CII thus, can reduce assessees' actual tax liability, especially when their sale price payments for long-term gains, and when the seller refuses to invest in other financial instruments.
