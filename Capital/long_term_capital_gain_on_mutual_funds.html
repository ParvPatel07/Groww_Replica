Long Term Capital Gain on Mutual Funds

Mutual Funds are a type of investment scheme in which funds are pooled from various investors and invested in bonds, stocks, and company shares. The Security and Exchange Board of India regulates the market, known as SEBI.

Professional fund managers manage funds and investors collectively to fetch long-term capital gains on Mutual Funds or short-term capital gains on higher-risk shares.

Mutual Funds offer two types of returns: capital gains and dividends. A capital gain indicates the difference between the cost of purchase and the net asset value at the time of selling units.

Capital Gains on Mutual Funds

Capital gains are gains when individuals/investors from the capital appreciation of a security or selling or transferring shares that are purchased. A Mutual Fund manager provides the report on appreciation when selling a unit or realizing the asset from the investment.

These capital gains are classified into two types, depending on the holding period â€“ long-term capital assets and short-term capital assets. The holding period is the duration for which the asset is held before it is sold or transferred. If Mutual Fund units are held for more than 36 months, they are treated as long-term capital assets. If held for less than 36 months, they are considered short-term capital assets. Similarly, for equity-oriented funds, the holding period is 12 months. If you sell before 12 months, they are considered short-term capital assets.

In the case of unlisted equity shares, debt funds, and other specified balanced funds, if the holding period is longer than 36 months, it is considered a long-term capital asset.

Types of Capital Assets on Mutual Funds

There are two types of capital assets on Mutual Funds: equity-oriented funds and debt funds. Any asset such as equity shares, equity-oriented mutual funds, and units of business trusts are equity-oriented funds if more than 65% of the total proceeds are invested in equity shares of domestic companies.

Similarly, any capital asset that is not an equity-oriented fund is a debt-oriented fund. If the holding period is less than 12 months, any gain is considered a short-term capital gain. If the holding period is more than 12 months, it is considered a long-term capital gain.

For listed equity shares and equity-oriented funds, the cost of acquisition as of 31st Jan 2018 (grandfathering) is the highest price quoted on the recognized stock exchange on that date.

What is an Indexed Cost of Acquisition?

The indexed cost of acquisition is the cost of acquisition adjusted for inflation, also known as indexation. The reason behind indexation is to reduce the tax liability on long-term capital gains. The cost inflation index is notified by the government every year.

The indexed cost of acquisition for capital gains on Mutual Funds considers the cost of asset as indexed value.
