National Savings Scheme (NSS)

Typically, national savings schemes are savings instruments launched or backed by the government and operated through post offices and public sector banks. These schemes offer investors assured returns with minimal risk. National savings schemes aim to mobilize small savings and help individuals build a substantial corpus eventually. Further, the rates of return under such schemes are often higher than traditional bank fixed deposits.

Most of the national savings schemes can be categorised based on the beneficiary categories they are directed to. Each of such schemes comes with pre-fixed eligibility criteria and offers an array of features and benefits. Hence, before making any investment under these schemes, investors must look into their profitable schemes in detail.

Types of National Saving Schemes

For Regular Investors

  • Post Office Monthly Income Scheme

    This small savings scheme is quite similar to the savings account held with banks. Through this savings scheme, investors can generate a fixed income monthly, which accounts are held with banks based on deposits held for a tenure of 5 years.

    Only resident Indians can invest in the scheme at their resident post office with a minimum investment of Rs. 1000. Joint accounts can be opened by up to 3 adults and for minors above 10 years of age. Individuals are also allowed to open a Post Office Monthly Income account jointly with 2 or 3 applicants, whereby the minimum amount per account is Rs. 15 Lakh. A lumpsum amount is pre-deposited by the government and is returned annually.

  • Post Office Recurring Deposit Account

    Under this national savings scheme, investors are required to deposit a predetermined amount of money each month for a tenure of 5 years. The minimum amount that can be put in is Rs. 100, and there is no upper limit on this. Investors also have access to an anytime option to pay fees for a significant return amount.

    For instance, Mr Ashok invests Rs. 1000 into this scheme for nine months which accrues interest at the rate of 7.2% p.a. On scheme completion, the maturity amount from this national savings scheme would be Rs. 9,000.

  • Post Office Savings Account and Post Office Time Deposit Account

    The Post Office Savings Account is quite similar to the regular savings account in banks, except that such an account can be opened at a post office. Parents and legal guardians can also open the said account on behalf of a minor. Here, the interest on deposits up to Rs. 10,000 per year is tax-free, while interest earned on deposits up to Rs. 1000 in a financial year, however, has no such tax rebates are offered on the accrued interest.

    On the other hand, the Post Office Time Deposit Account is similar to a fixed deposit and can be opened with a minimum deposit of Rs. 200.

  • Public Provident Fund

    This small savings scheme aims to help mobilize small savings but further helps to build a reliable corpus over the years. Resident Indians can open a PPF account at selected financial institutions or a post office with a minimum deposit of Rs. 500 each year. Typically, PPF accounts come with a tenure of 15 years which can be further extended for up to 5 years.

    One cannot open more than one account under this national savings scheme. The rate of returns offered under this scheme is paid annually, annually and compounded on the account's balance between the 5th and 6th day of the month.

    Further, individuals can avail up to 25% of loan against their deposit between the 3rd and 6th year of their account.

  • Kisan Vikas Patra

    It is typically a scheme that can be availed with the issue of certificate from a post office and helps generate a reasonable return. The amount invested under this scheme will double in 124 months (10 years and 4 months), and investors can benefit from a fixed interest rate annually. The returns also help to meet their financial goals through investing in this scheme. Individuals can jointly open this KVP account with up to 3 adults, but not NRIs or HUF. Notably, individuals can invest in this savings scheme on behalf of minors as well.

    If an individual needs to avail of loan against their Kisan Vikas Patra certificate under certain circumstances, the Kisan Vikas Patra can further be used as collateral to avail secured loans from financial institutions.

  • National Savings Certificate

    It is a fixed-income-generating investment scheme, whereby account can be opened at any post office both individually and jointly. This scheme aims to help encourage small and income groups to mobilise their savings through investments while encouraging tax saving.

    Typically, the investment option does not have any maximum limit on investment and comes with two distinct maturity periods of five and ten years. Investors can avail income tax benefits under Section 80C from financial institutions. However, NRIs, HUFs and trusts are not allowed to invest in this savings scheme.

For Senior Citizens

  • Senior Citizens Savings Scheme (SCSS)

    It is one of the most popular national savings schemes and is considered a viable option for ensuring financial independence for senior citizens. After reaching 60 years of age, this highly-acclaimed saving scheme for senior citizens makes it a profitable investment option.

    Any residing Indians over 60 years of age would be eligible to avail this scheme that comes with a maturity period of 5 years, which can be further extended for 3 years. Individuals can also avail income tax benefits up to Rs 1.5 Lakh under the 80C, and the facility of Senior Citizens Savings Scheme interest earned is tax-free.

For Girl Child

  • Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana

    This small savings scheme is directed towards girl children of India 10 years of age or younger. It is one of the most profitable small savings schemes as individuals can open an account under the scheme with a minimum of Rs. 250.

    Notably, such an account can be opened by the parents or legal guardians of the girl child at the nearest post office or branch of participating financial institutions. The rate of interest on this scheme is revised quarterly.

    Account holders can resort to the premature withdrawal facility only in case of funding the beneficiary's higher education or wedding and withdraw up to 50% of the total deposit. Notably, such withdrawals are allowed for meeting the expenses of higher education of the beneficiary.

    Since the importance of NSS in building a robust fund is undeniable, investors should be careful while opting for a suitable savings scheme. Further, to make the most of these national savings schemes, individuals should take into account their investor eligibility, investment requirement and available savings for an investment.

Common Features and Benefits of these National Savings Schemes

Here's a list of common features and benefits of these national savings schemes:

  • Assured Returns

    Returns on the NSS schemes are declared before investment and are assured. Also, the fact that they are not linked to the market fluctuations, helps investors to avoid risk. This, in turn, encourages them to save more.

  • Safety

    Since the government backs the schemes, the risk of losing one's principal investment or incurring a loss is eliminated. Such a vital feature allows both risk-averse investors and beginners to adopt a responsible approach towards saving money without fear of losing it.

  • Tax Benefits

    Most National Savings Schemes come with potential tax-saving opportunities under Section 80C. Such benefits tend to inculcate financial discipline and further encourage individuals to save more.

  • Substantial Returns

    Usually, the rate of return on these savings plans is adjusted quarterly. It is mostly done to help individuals earn inflation-adjusted returns and make the most of their investments.

Notably, the features and advantages may vary depending on the type of national scheme one has opted for. Consequently, it is essential to become familiar with the different types of National Savings Schemes to pick the most suitable one.
