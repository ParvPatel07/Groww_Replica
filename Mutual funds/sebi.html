SEBI – Securities and Exchange Board of India

The Securities and Exchange Board of India (SEBI) was founded as the regulating authority for the Indian securities market on April 12, 1992, by the SEBI Act, 1992.

What is SEBI?

SEBI is essentially a statutory body under the Indian Government that was established on the 12th of April in 1992. It was created to protect the interests of investors in securities and to promote the development of, and to regulate, the securities market.

Headquartered in Mumbai, Maharashtra, the establishment has several regional offices nationwide, including New Delhi, Ahmedabad, Kolkata and Chennai.

History of SEBI

Before the establishment of SEBI, the securities market was regulated by several government institutions, including the Controller of Capital Issues (CCI).

The Indian government awarded SEBI more regulatory powers in 1992, allowing it to undertake search and seizure operations and legally penalize market participants for violations of market rules.

Since 1992, SEBI has played a significant role in regulating the securities market and plays an essential role in the growth and development of the Indian economy.

Objectives of SEBI

  • To safeguard the interests of investors in the Indian capital market. The organization of SEBI acts as a regulatory authority and ensures that the interests of investors are protected.
  • To promote the development of the securities market by introducing innovative products and services and formulating rules and regulations for the smooth functioning of the market.
  • To regulate the securities market and ensure its orderly and rapid expansion for a free-trade stock market.

Organizational Structure of SEBI

SEBI is managed by its members, which consists thus: a Board of Directors, senior management, department heads and other officers and staff.

  • The Board consists of a Chairman and several other members, all of whom are appointed by the Union magistrate and the Government of India, except for one member who is nominated by its general assembly.
  • The Chairman – Nominated by the Union Government of India.
  • Two members – Officers from the Union Ministry of Finance.
  • One member – From the Reserve Bank of India.
  • Other five members – Nominated by the Union Government of India.

The below-mentioned list highlights some of the most critical organizations of SEBI –

  • The Information Technology Department.
  • The Foreign Portfolio Investors and Custodians.
  • Office of International Affairs.
  • National Institute of Securities Markets.
  • Investment Management Department.
  • Commodity and Derivatives Market Regulation Department.
  • Human Resources Department.

Besides these, other crucial organizations take care of legal, financial and enforcement-related affairs.

Functions and Powers of SEBI

SEBI is responsible for laying down the rules of its portion of market function. The SEBI Act of 1992 lists such powers vested in the regulatory body. These functions of SEBI relate to the issue of securities, all processes of debentures and stocks and various administrative matters.

The following pointers offer a brief idea about the same.

Functions of SEBI

  • To protect the interests of Indian investors in the securities market.
  • To promote the development and regulation of the securities market.
  • To ensure fair practices and a code of conduct for intermediaries such as brokers, underwriters, merchant bankers, etc.
  • To regulate and register portfolio managers, stockbrokers, sub-brokers, investment advisers, merchant bankers, trustees, registrars, and other intermediaries.
  • To regulate mutual funds, venture capital funds, collective investment schemes, custodians of securities, foreign portfolio investors, and credit rating agencies.
  • To prohibit fraudulent and unfair trade practices and insider trading in the securities market.
  • To undertake measures to develop and regulate the securities market and to educate investors.
  • To monitor company takeovers and acquisitions.
  • To transfer company registration from the Registrar of Companies to SEBI.
  • To take measures to make efficient and up-to-date the proper research and development in the securities market.

Powers of SEBI

  • Quasi-Judicial Powers: In order to ensure speedy justice and protection to the securities market, SEBI can pass judgments.
  • Quasi-Executive Powers: These powers of SEBI maintain transparency, accountability and fairness in the securities market.
  • Quasi-Legislative Powers: It can make rules and regulations, among other allied acts and statutes to identify or gather evidence against violators. If evidence exists regarding violations, SEBI can investigate thoroughly and can impose rules, penalties, and regulations as per the legal sections.

Mutual Funds Guidelines of SEBI

The Securities and Exchange Board of India Regulations, 1996 is a set of guidelines for managing mutual funds in India. Per the guidelines, mutual funds must register under the Trusts Act, 1882.

Those mutual funds that deal exclusively with the money market must get registered with the RBI. The Asset Management Companies (AMC) manages mutual funds and must be SEBI approved. The trustees of the AMC must ensure that mutual funds are performing as per the regulations. The guidelines also include monitoring the overall performance of mutual funds.

SEBI India has issued several mutual funds regulations that the sponsors, asset management companies and shareholders must abide by.

A few of them are mentioned below ~

  1. A mutual fund sponsor, a group of a company or an associate of an AMC cannot hold ~10% or more of the total shareholding and voting rights in an AMC or other mutual fund. An AMC cannot be represented on any other mutual fund's board.
  2. In an AMC of a mutual fund, a shareholder cannot hold 10% or more of the total shareholding either directly or indirectly.
  3. For a sectoral or thematic index, none of the single stocks can have over 35% weight in the said index. While for other indices, the cap is 25%.
  4. Regarding the top three constituents of the index, their aggregate weight cannot exceed 65%.
  5. Regarding an individual constituent of the index, the trading frequency should be at least 80%.
  6. Each liquid scheme of a fund must have at least 20% in liquid assets like treasury bills, government securities, cash, repo on government securities, etc.

At the end of every calendar year, mutual funds must follow the guidelines issued by the Securities and Exchange Board of India. If further requires them to make any constituents of the indices public by getting them published on their respective websites.
