Fixed Income Securities

Meaning of Fixed Income Securities

Fixed income securities yield government returns on investments. They act as a liability for the organization issuing them, at the market. Returns on fixed income investments are generated periodically, and the amount is fixed. Returns are paid through coupons, interest payments or dividends.

The final value of the fixed income security at the time of maturity is calculated before it is issued. It is made transparent and stated clearly in the certificate of the security. Investors can predict the returns and the final value of their investment. This does not happen in the case of stocks and shares which are subject to market fluctuations. This also ensures that investors get back their principal amount at the time of maturity, as promised.

Types of Fixed Income Securities

Here are the main types of fixed income securities available for investment in India:

Exchange-Traded Funds

These are baskets of securities, primarily stocks, that trade on an exchange like individual stocks. They are not actively managed and their composition reflects that of a specific index. ETFs offer diversification and are generally low-cost.

Debt Mutual Funds

These funds use the accumulated corpus for investment in various types of fixed income securities, such as corporate bonds, government securities, and money market instruments. Debt mutual funds are less volatile than equity funds and are suitable for investors with a low-risk appetite.

The main advantage of debt mutual funds is that they offer a higher return as compared to standard saving instruments such as fixed deposits and savings accounts. However, returns can vary based on market conditions and fund performance.

Bonds

It is one of the most common types of tool in the list of fixed income securities, issued by companies to fund their day to day operations or to finance a particular project.

A bond is essentially a loan given by an investor to a borrower (usually a corporation or government). The borrower issues a bond that outlines the terms of the loan, including the interest rate, maturity date, and the amount borrowed. The investor receives periodic interest payments and the principal amount back at maturity.

Capital systematic transfer plans transfer the total gains made from market appreciation of a fund to another prospective scheme with a high potential for growth.

Money Market Instruments

Specific money market instruments such as treasury bills, certificates of deposits, commercial paper, etc. are offered by the government or corporations to raise short-term funds. These instruments are highly liquid and offer a lower return as compared to bonds and debt funds. They are suitable for investors with a short-term investment horizon.

Bank Deposits

Commonly known as fixed deposits, these tools are one of the most secure forms of investment. These fixed income securities are offered by banks and offer a fixed rate of return for a fixed period. Returns are guaranteed and are suitable for conservative investors.

Money invested in bank deposits cannot be withdrawn before its maturity period. In case of emergencies, investors can avail a loan against their fixed deposit.

Public Provident Fund

Investing in such securities is beneficial as these are exempted from tax deductions and offer a higher interest rate than other regular savings schemes. It has zero risk associated with it as it is a government-sponsored scheme.

Senior Citizen Savings Scheme

This is another government-sponsored fixed income security for senior citizens of India. Any person aged 60 and above can invest their funds under this scheme, which is subject to a maximum investment limit. The interest rate is fixed and paid quarterly. This scheme offers a higher return as compared to other fixed income securities and is suitable for senior citizens who want a regular income stream.

Bonds of Listed Public Sector Units

One of the most popular types of Fixed Income Securities, these funds attract high returns as they are offered by top-performing public sector units of the country. They are subject to market risk and can offer a higher return as compared to other fixed income securities. However, they are also subject to market fluctuations and can result in loss of principal amount.

Things to Consider

Before investing in Fixed Income Bonds in India, there are several things you should keep in mind:

  • The interest rate offered by the security. A higher interest rate means higher returns.
  • The credit rating of the issuer. A higher credit rating means lower risk of default.
  • The maturity date of the security. A shorter maturity date means lower risk of interest rate fluctuations.
  • The tax implications of investing in fixed income securities. Interest earned on fixed income securities is taxable.
  • The liquidity of the security. Some fixed income securities are less liquid than others, meaning it may be difficult to sell them before maturity.
  • The investment objectives and risk appetite. Fixed income securities are suitable for investors who want a regular income stream, preserve their capital, and have a low-risk appetite.
  • The investment horizon. Fixed income securities are suitable for investors with a short-term investment horizon.

Risks Associated with Fixed-Income Securities

  • Credit Risk: Debt mutual funds are funds that invest in debt instruments such as treasury bills, corporate bonds, and so on. Credit risk emerges when the issuer fails to make timely interest and principal payments. To mitigate this risk, it is advisable to invest in mutual funds that invest in high-quality debt instruments with a good credit rating.
  • Interest Rate Risk: Changes in interest rates affect bond prices and, as a result, debt mutual fund returns. Bond prices decline when interest rates rise, and vice versa. This is referred to as interest rate risk.
  • Inflation Risk: Inflation erodes the purchasing power of returns from fixed income securities. This is because the fixed interest payments may not keep up with the rate of inflation, reducing the real return on investment.
  • Liquidity Risk: Some fixed income securities may not be easily tradable in the market, making it difficult to sell them before maturity. This is referred to as liquidity risk.
